# The Two‑Witness Context Argument (Romans 3:20–28)

> #### 📄 Abstract
> Paul’s meaning in Romans 3:28 becomes unmistakable once you read it alongside its “prequel,” Romans 3:20–22. In that earlier passage, Paul uses the technical biblical formula “the law and the prophets,” which always refers to the **Torah** and the **Prophets**—the first two sections of the Jewish Scriptures. This locks the meaning of *nomos* (“law”) in place. If “LAW” in verse 21 means the **Torah** (and it does), then the same word in verse 20 must also mean the **Torah**. And if it means the **Torah** in verse 20, it must mean the **Torah** in verse 28. In other words, “works of law” in Romans 3:28 does not mean “good works” or “moral effort.” It means **works of the Mosaic Law**—the covenantal identity markers of Judaism. Paul’s point is not that human effort is irrelevant, but that Jewish covenantal privilege does not justify anyone. Jews and Gentiles alike are justified by faith, apart from the works of the **Torah**.

The second of the four arguments we are going to consider is the **Two‑Witness Context Argument**.

This is another contextual argument from Romans. But unlike the **Covenant‑Context Argument**—which took a big-picture approach to Romans 1–3—the **Two‑Witness Context Argument** zooms in on the immediate context of Romans 3:28 to show, yet again, that when Paul speaks of “works of law,” he unambiguously means **works of the Mosaic Law**.

Let’s get to it.


## Introduction to the Two‑Witness Context Argument

Our focus is once more on Romans 3:28:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of law.

We have already noted that the Greek word *nomos* (“law”) can refer either to:

- a **moral standard** (Protestant interpretation), or  
- the **Torah** / **Mosaic Law** (Catholic interpretation).

But which meaning does Paul have in mind?

Earlier, I argued that the broader context of Romans 1–3 supports the Catholic reading:

- Paul contrasts Jew/Gentile, circumcised/uncircumcised, and those under the **LAW**/without the **LAW**. 
- His critique targets **covenantal entitlement**, not moral legalism.  
- He treats circumcision—the first and defining work of the Mosaic Law—as a **covenantal identity marker**.

Thus, the covenantal context strongly supports the conclusion that “works of law” in Romans 3:28 means **works of the Torah**.

But this conclusion doesn’t depend on a single line of contextual reasoning.

You see, there is a *prequel* passage in Romans 3 that speaks both of “faith” and “works of law”. And what it tells us about Paul’s intended meaning is profound. 


## Romans 3:20–22: The Prequel to 3:28

Romans 3:20–22 is a passage that receives surprisingly little attention in Protestant circles, even though it is essentially a clone of Romans 3:28.

Verse 20 explicitly states that no one is justified by “works of the law”:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:20 (RSV‑2CE)  
> For no human being will be justified in his sight by works of the law…

Verses 21–22 then affirm that righteousness comes “through faith in Jesus Christ”:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:21–22 (RSV‑2CE)  
> The righteousness of God has been manifested apart from **LAW**, although the **LAW** and the prophets bear witness to it, the righteousness of God through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe...

Taken together, the passage gives us justification **through faith** apart from **works of the law**.

The passage is as close to a first‑century copy‑and‑paste as you can get. Romans 3:20–22 effectively gives us *everything* that Romans 3:28 does—*and more*.  

But as we shall see, it is precisely that “*and more*“ that creates tension for Protestant interpreters.


## The Law and the Prophets

You may have noticed that when I cited Romans 3:20, I did **not** render “works of the law” as “works of the **LAW**.” I promised to reserve that formatting for cases where the meaning is unambiguously the **Torah**.

But note that in verse 21, I did render “law” as “**LAW**“:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:21 (RSV‑2CE)  
> The righteousness of God has been manifested apart from **LAW**, although the **LAW** and the prophets bear witness to it... 

Here, “LAW” unquestionably means the **Torah**. How do we know? Because Paul uses the technical biblical formula **“the law and the prophets.”**

**This is Paul’s tell.**


## The Law and the Prophets (Explained)

As [previously noted](./c004-saint-paul-works-of-law.md), the Jewish Scriptures were divided into three major sections:

1. **Torah**  
2. **Prophets**  
3. **Writings**

Notice the first two canonical groups: the **Torah** and the **Prophets**. 

Here is what you need to know: Whenever Scripture uses the phrase **“the law and the prophets,”** it is a technical formula that *always* means **the Torah and the Prophets**.

The phrase, or a variant of it, appears seven other times in the New Testament. In every instance, “**LAW**“ means **Torah**.[^Law-and-Prophets]

[^Law-and-Prophets]: The New Testament usage includes: 
(1) Matthew 5:17—“I have not come to abolish the **LAW** and the prophets…”
(2) Matthew 7:12—“…for this is the **LAW** and the prophets.”
(3) Matthew 11:13—“For all the prophets and the **LAW** prophesied until John.”
(4) Matthew 22:40—“On these two commandments depend all the **LAW** and the prophets.”
(5) Luke 16:16—“The **LAW** and the prophets were until John.”
(6) Acts 13:15—“After the reading of the **LAW** and the prophets…”
(7) Acts 24:14—“…believing everything laid down by the **LAW** and written in the prophets.”
In all instances the word “**LAW**“ (Greek *nomos*) unambiguously means **Torah**.

**There are no exceptions.**


## The Two‑Witness Rule

But what does the phrase **“the LAW and the prophets”** mean?

It invokes the Deuteronomic **two‑witness rule** (Deut 17:6; 19:15).

Under the Mosaic Law, truth could be established only on the corroborating testimony of **two independent witnesses**.

Thus, when the New Testament speaks of “**the LAW and the prophets**,” it is invoking that same principle. When both the **Torah** and the **Prophets** testify to the same truth, the matter is established **beyond doubt**.

So when Paul writes:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:21 (RSV‑2CE)
> The righteousness of God has been manifested apart from **LAW**, although the **LAW** and the prophets bear witness to it.

he is saying two things:

1. **God’s saving righteousness has now been revealed in Christ in a way that does not depend on the Mosaic Law or its works.**
2. **This revelation is fully confirmed by both the Torah and the Prophets, giving it decisive scriptural authority.**

If you want a good analogy—imagine a child asking both parents for something they really want. One “no” would be bad—although there might still be wiggle room. But two “no’s”? Even the most determined child knows when the gig is up.

The “law and the prophets” formula has the same weight. When you see it in Scripture, it’s time to pull out the highlighter; the Bible is about to drop some wisdom.[^Romans-4-Arugment]

[^Romans-4-Arugment]: Side note: When Paul says “the **LAW** and the Prophets bear witness” to this new manifestation of righteousness, he is teasing a biblical proof that will come in Romans 4. It is truly a *exquisite* argument proving definitively that the Torah—the very source of Jewish boasting and ethnic identity—states that man is truly justified by faith and not by doing the works of the Torah. The argument that Paul presents in Romans 4 is mic-drop good—a true 10 out of 10 winner. But there is a catch. The proof that Paul uses only works if “works of the law” means **works of the Mosaic Law**. It completely breaks if we try to sneak **good works** under the umbrella of what Paul is calling “works of the law.” We will review this argument in an [upcoming chapter](./c009-the-abraham-timeline-argument.md).


## Romans 3:20–22: The Prequel to 3:28 (Part II)

Now that we know what Paul means by “the law and the prophets,” let’s return to Romans 3:20–22:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:21 (RSV‑2CE)  
> The righteousness of God has been manifested apart from **LAW** [**Torah**], although the **LAW** [**Torah**] and the **Prophets** bear witness to it...

Recall our **RIGHT** rules:

- **RIGHT‑1**: We must define biblical words and expression using their **biblical definition**.
- **RIGHT‑2**: The interpretation of a verse **must agree with its context**.

Both rules are again in play.  

In verse 21, “**LAW**“ = **Torah**. No one argues the point. To quote the Borg: “Resistance is futile.”

In verse 20—*the preceding verse*—we have a contextually related use of the word “law”:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:20 (RSV‑2CE)  
> For no human being will be justified in his sight by works of the law

Can we reasonably suppose that “law” here means something *other* than the **Torah**?

To argue, as many Protestant theologians do, that Paul means “good works” is to reject both **RIGHT** rules: to ignore the biblical definition of *nomos* and to sever verse 20 from its context.

If applying the rules breaks your theology, the problem is probably not the rules. The Bible means what the Bible means—inconvenient as that sometimes is.

If **RIGHT** is right, then verse 20 must be read as:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:20 (RSV‑2CE)  
> For no human being will be justified in his sight by works of the **LAW** [**Torah**]

And once that is granted, the conclusion for verse 28 follows:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)  
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of **LAW** [the **Torah**].


## Conclusion

There is no interpretive escape hatch. If *nomos* means **Torah** in verse 21, it means **Torah** in verse 20. And if it means **Torah** in verse 20, it must mean **Torah** in verse 28.

To maintain consistency, one must either claim:

- Romans 3:21 says, “the righteousness of God has been manifested apart from **good works**, although **good works** and the prophets bear witness to it,” **-OR-**
- Romans 3:28 says, “man is justified by faith apart from works of the **Torah / Mosaic Law**.”

One of those readings is nonsensical—and I doubt I have to tell you which one it is. 

But if you believe Romans 3:28 teaches the Protestant doctrine of *sola fide*, then you are effectively married to that nonsensical reading of Romans 3:21.[^Law-Shift]

[^Law-Shift]: Protestants sometimes argue that Paul uses “law” (Greek *nomos*) in multiple senses and that context does not force a single meaning. That is true—up to a point. Paul’s use of “law” in Romans 2:14—“When Gentiles who have not the law do by nature what the law requires, they are a law to themselves, even though they do not have the law”—does not govern its meaning in Romans 3:20. No argument there. But Paul’s usage of “law” in Romans 3:21 **does** govern its meaning within Paul’s single, continuous thought in Romans 3:20–22. Paul can use *nomos* flexibly, but **he does not shift meanings mid‑argument without clear contextual markers**. Romans 3:20–22 contains none. Anyone claiming a semantic shift here bears the burden of proof.


Applying the **RIGHT** rules, the Catholic interpretation naturally follows.

The expression “works of law” in Romans 3:28 is not a cipher for human effort. It is a shorthand for the Jewish Torah.[^Works-Of-Law-Split]

[^Works-Of-Law-Split]: A common Protestant move is to concede that “law” (Greek *nomos*) in Romans 3:21 refers to the **Torah**, while arguing that the phrase “works of law” refers more broadly to **moral works**. This is typically supported by noting that the Mosaic Law contains moral commands (e.g., the Decalogue) alongside ritual and ceremonial prescriptions. It is certainly true that the Torah includes moral requirements. But this does not establish that Paul is redefining a fixed Second Temple expression. The phrase “works of the law” (*ma’ase ha‑torah*) appears in the Dead Sea Scrolls, most notably in **4QMMT** (“Some of the Works of the Law”). There, the expression refers specifically to **ritual, purity, and covenant‑boundary observances**—the concrete practices that marked out the Qumran community’s interpretation of the Mosaic Law. There is no evidence in 4QMMT that *ma’ase ha‑torah* includes general moral codes. The historical record shows that “works of the law” was already an established technical expression in Second Temple Judaism with a **defined semantic range**. Paul did not coin the phrase; he inherited it. And his usage aligns with the meaning attested in his Jewish contemporaries.

Paul’s point is clear: 

**Justification is not based on Jewish covenantal identity or privilege. Jews and Gentiles alike are justified by faith.**


## Final Thoughts

The **Two‑Witness Argument** is another contextual argument—and contextual arguments sometimes get a bad rap. They are circumstantial by nature and leave a bit of interpretive wiggle room.

But that does not mean they lack force. This one carries real weight. And paired with the **Covenant‑Context Argument**, the Catholic case becomes quite compelling. 

Both the broad context of Romans 1–3 and the immediate context of Romans 3:20–22 converge on the same conclusion: “works of law” means **works of the Torah**.

Still, if you are looking for a smoking gun, we are not there yet. That degree of evidence is coming.

They are both veritable **debate enders**.
