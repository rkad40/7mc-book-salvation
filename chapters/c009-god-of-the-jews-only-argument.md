# The God‑of‑the‑Jews‑Only Argument (Romans 3:28–29)

> #### 📄 Abstract
> Romans 3:28 is often quoted in isolation as a proof‑text for *sola fide*, but Paul immediately follows it with a logical contrast in Romans 3:29. When the two verses are read together, Paul creates an “A‑or‑B” structure: either man is justified by faith apart from “works of law,” **or** God is only the God of the Jews. This forces “works of law” to refer to something uniquely Jewish—namely, the **Mosaic boundary markers** that distinguished Jews from Gentiles. When we apply the BYOD (Bring Your Own Definition) test from **RIGHT‑1**, only the Catholic definition (“works of the Mosaic Law”) makes the logic coherent. The Protestant definition (“good works”) collapses the argument. This chapter shows that Romans 3:28–29, read in context, unambiguously refers to **works of the Mosaic Law**, not good works.

The **Covenant‑Context Argument** and the **Two‑Witness Context Argument** are strong proofs, but admittedly, they do leave a bit of wiggle room.

The next proof, the **God‑of‑the‑Jews‑Only Argument**, is a *much* better and *far* stronger argument. This argument once again leans heavily into our **RIGHT** rules. It involves context (**RIGHT‑2**)—but in a way that removes subjectivity from the analysis.

Let’s jump right in.


## Introduction to the God‑of‑the‑Jews‑Only Argument

It is not uncommon to hear Protestant apologists cite or paraphrase Romans 3:28 without commentary, as if the obvious meaning aligns with *sola fide*.

For example, one of my favorite Protestant authors, Dr. R. C. Sproul, does exactly this in his book *Are We Together? A Protestant Analyzes Roman Catholicism*. He writes that Catholics are guilty of:

> “ignoring many plain teachings of Scripture, such as Romans 3:28: ‘For we hold that man is justified by faith apart from works of the law.’”[^1]

Sproul quotes, but does not even try to interpret, Romans 3:28. Why would he? According to Sproul, this is one of the “plain teachings of Scripture.”[^1] **It needs no interpretation.** 

[^1]: R. C. Sproul, *Are We Together? A Protestant Analyzes Roman Catholicism*, p. 3.

Most Protestants share this view. Romans 3:28 teaches *sola fide*. Period.

But is that really how it works?

Sometimes our biases are just biased. Our contextual analysis of Romans 1–3 should at least open the door to some warranted skepticism.


## The Bat‑in‑the‑Attic Example

Let’s do a little thought experiment. Suppose I say to you:

> “There is a bat in my attic.”

What do you think that means? 

The word “bat” can mean two different things:

- a rodent of the winged nocturnal variety **–OR–**
- the club‑like instrument a baseball player uses to mash a baseball.

Which is it?

Before you can ask for clarification, I add:

> “That’s where I keep all my old baseball equipment.”

Mystery solved. No need to call pest control. All you needed to decode my meaning was **one extra sentence of context**.

Sometimes it really is that simple.  


## Adding Just One Verse of Context

Let’s lean into **RIGHT-2** and add just one more verse of context to Romans 3:28 in the form of the very next verse:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28–29 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of law. **Or** is God the God of the Jews only?

Note the “or” wedged between verses 28 and 29. That’s a *very* important word. It tells us that Paul is making a logical A‑or‑B connection between the two verses. Either:

> `A`. Man is justified by faith apart from works of law **–OR–**  
> `B`. God is only the God of the Jews.

In Boolean logic[^Boolean] notation, we would write:

> `A OR B`

[^Boolean]: Boolean logic is the basic true/false structure behind everyday reasoning and all modern computers. It works by combining statements with “and,” “or,” and “not” to show what follows from what. Without Boolean logic, we wouldn’t have digital technology, and many philosophical and scientific disciplines would lack the clear, testable framework they use to analyze arguments, build models, and evaluate evidence. It provides a reliable, rule‑bound way of discerning and testing truth claims within any system that depends on definite yes/no conditions.

For a simple `A OR B` relationship, think about flipping a coin. The result can only be:

> `A`. Heads **–OR–**  
> `B`. Tails

Such that:

- If you land `A` heads, you didn’t land `B` tails.  
- If you land `B` tails, you didn’t land `A` heads.

There are two **logically possible outcomes**, only one of which will be realized.

In Boolean notation, the possibilities are:

> 1. `If A then B̅`
> 2. `If A̅ then B`

…where `A̅` means “not `A`” and `B̅` means “not `B`.”

However you express it, the logic is **inviolate** regardless of what values we assign to `A` and `B`. Only one of them **can** be true; the corresponding value **must** be false.[^XOR]

[^XOR]: In classical Boolean logic, the `OR` operation in `A OR B` is true if *either* `A` or `B` is true, including the case where *both* are true. This is not the sense in which Paul is using “or.” He is using it in a **disjunctive** sense, where either `A` or `B` is true—*but not both*. In computer architecture and coding (my discipline), we distinguish these two forms by annotating the inclusive case as `OR` and the exclusive case as `XOR`, which stands for **EXCLUSIVE‑OR**. Romans 3:28–29 uses the `XOR` form. I am not rendering it that way because `XOR` is niche to the computer‑science domain, but I have taken care to spell out Paul’s usage in logical notation.

Boolean logic is refreshingly uncompromising in this way—it doesn’t negotiate, it doesn’t hedge, and it never says “Well, it depends.”


## The Logical OR of Romans 3:28–29

We can apply this same logical synthesis to Romans 3:28–29:

> 1. If (`A`) man is justified by faith apart from works of law, then (`B̅`) God is **NOT** only the God of the Jews.
> 2. If (`A̅`) man is **NOT** justified by faith apart from works of law, then (`B`) God *is* only the God of the Jews.

We don’t have to wonder which of these two possible options is correct. Paul tells us. In the remainder of Romans 3:29, he says:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:29 (RSV‑2CE)
> Is not God the God of the Gentiles also? Yes, of the Gentiles also.

So **Option 1** is the correct formula for justification.

But here is the critical point: **Both formulas are logically valid**.

Even though we know **Option 1** is correct, **Option 2** is still logically coherent. If, in fact, it *were* the case that man was justified by works of law, **then God *would* only be the God of the Jews**.

Let me repeat that in case the argument failed to land the first time:

> #### Romans 3:28–29 (If `A̅` then `B` Form)  
> If man is justified by works of law, **then God is only the God of the Jews**.

It necessarily follows that—whatever Paul means by “works of law”—he must have in mind something that applies **only to Jews**.

This leaves us with only one option: “works of law” in Romans 3:28 unambiguously refers to **works of the Mosaic Law**.


## Applying the BYOD Test

Not buying it yet? 

Let’s dig into our bag of **RIGHT** tricks and dust off the **BYOD** test (**B**ring **Y**our **O**wn **D**efinition). To apply this test, we take the statement:

> #### Romans 3:28–29 (If `A̅` then `B` Form)  
> If man is justified by works of law, then God is only the God of the Jews.

…and substitute the Catholic and Protestant definitions of “works of law.” If your interpretation is correct, it must—at a bare minimum—make sense of Paul’s dogmatic statement.

### Catholic Definition: Works of the Mosaic Law

First up, the Catholic definition of “works of law”:

> #### Romans 3:28–29 (If `A̅` then `B` Form, Catholic Rendering)  
> If man is justified by **works of the Mosaic Law**—things like *circumcision*, *abstaining from pork*, and *keeping the holy days*—then God is only the God of the Jews.

Does this make sense? 

**Absolutely.**

If the only people who can be saved are those who are first circumcised and then keep all the Mosaic laws that uniquely define Jewish identity, then God would, in fact, only be the God of the Jews.

### Protestant Definition: Good Works

Next up, the Protestant definition:

> #### Romans 3:28–29 (If `A̅` then `B` Form, Protestant Rendering)  
> If man is justified by **good works**—things like *feeding the hungry*, *clothing the naked*, *visiting the sick*—then God is only the God of the Jews.

Does that make sense? 

**Absolutely not.**

How does pulling a five‑dollar bill out of my wallet and giving it to a man holding a cardboard sign that reads “HUNGRY” prove that God is only the God of the Jews?

![](../assets/img/pic/theme/system/HungrySign.svg)

It doesn’t.

It can’t.

Paul’s logic only works if “works of law” refers to Jewish covenantal identity markers—which is exactly what the covenant‑context of Romans has been telling us all along.


### “Works of Law” Means “Works of the Mosaic Law”

Only the Catholic definition makes sense of Paul’s logic. The Protestant definition does not.

And it is not the fault of the logical reduction itself. You can see the same thing in the unfiltered Text:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28–29 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of law. **Or** is God the God of the Jews only?

The logical reduction simply makes the conclusion **irrefutably clear**.[^Or-Conjunction]

[^Or-Conjunction]: The most common strategy employed by Protestant interpreters to sidestep the force of the “or” (Greek **ἤ**) in Romans 3:29 is what I call **The Ignore‑the‑Or Strategy**. Using this approach, one simply treats the “or” as if it were not there, or else functionally reinterprets it as a mere transitional conjunction. If verse 28 can be untethered from verse 29, then the two statements may be interpreted independently. This at least creates a pathway for claiming that verse 28 teaches justification by faith alone (understood as excluding all works), while verse 29 merely affirms that salvation is not ethnically restricted. But the conjunction **is** there, and its presence grammatically binds the two verses. Strong’s Concordance describes **ἤ** as “a primary particle of distinction between two **connected** terms.” In Koine Greek, **ἤ** is the standard disjunctive conjunction, introducing the second member of an **A OR B** structure. Paul uses **ἤ** in precisely this way—either to present a genuine disjunction or to introduce a rhetorical alternative that would follow if the preceding claim were false. Crucially, **ἤ never functions as a discourse‑level transitional particle** in Paul’s letters; he uses **δέ** (“but/now”) or **οὖν** (“therefore/so”) for transitions. **ἤ** always signals a disjunctive alternative. Paul’s usage follows a consistent rhetorical pattern: (1) He asserts a positive claim. (2) He introduces **ἤ** to pose an alternative that would follow if the claim were denied. (3) He expects the reader to reject the alternative as absurd, thereby reinforcing the original claim. This is classic Pauline logic: he forces the reader to choose between two incompatible propositions, with the false option always appearing as the second member (**B**) of the **A OR B** disjunction. Romans 3:28–29 fits this pattern exactly.

*One verse of context*—that’s all it took to unlock Paul’s meaning, turning a single verse that seemed ambiguous into something undeniably Catholic, consistent with what the Church has taught for two thousand years. 

Paul is not talking about “good works” or “works of righteousness.” He is talking about **works of the Mosaic Law**—the Jewish covenantal identity markers.

He is saying, in no uncertain terms, that man is justified by faith apart from **works of the Mosaic Law**—meaning you do not have to become a Jew in order to be a Christian.


## The Romans “Works of Law” BYOD Test

I know there will be objections. But unlike the previous two contextual arguments, the **God‑of‑the‑Jews‑Only Argument** leaves no logical backdoor. And here’s why:

Whatever your definition of “works of law” is, it **must** make sense within what I am calling the **Romans “Works of Law” BYOD Test**:

> #### Romans “Works of Law” BYOD Test (Fill‑in‑the‑Blank)  
> If man is justified by ____________________________, then God is only the God of the Jews.

Most theological debates are **subjective**. People bring their interpretations the way people bring opinions to social media—confidently, and sometimes without reading the original post. 

Invariably, people defend their interpretations because they *feel* true, or because they’ve *always believed them*, or because their *tradition depends on them*.

But the **Romans “Works of Law” BYOD Test** is emphatically **objective**.

It is not denominational. It does not bend to personal bias. It is **pure biblical logic applied in Paul’s own words**.

For your definition of “works of law” to be true, it **must** make Paul’s conditional statement true. If it doesn’t—if Paul’s logic breaks when you plug in your definition—**then the definition is wrong, along with any theology built on it**.

This test easily exposes interpretations that *cannot* be Paul’s, no matter how sincerely they are held.

And anyone who sincerely engages with this test will arrive at the same unavoidable conclusion: when Saint Paul speaks of “works of law” in Romans 3:28, he means **works of the Mosaic Law**. 

Paul’s logic is covenantal to the core—and once you see it, you can’t unsee it.
