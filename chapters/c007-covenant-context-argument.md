# The Covenant‑Context Argument (Romans 1:1–3:27)

> #### 📄 Abstract
> Paul’s argument in Romans 1–3 is not about people trying to earn salvation by good works.  It is about Jews claiming covenant privilege through the Mosaic Law—especially circumcision. Throughout these chapters, Paul contrasts Jew/Gentile, circumcised/uncircumcised, and those under the **LAW**/without the **LAW**. His target is not moral legalism but covenantal entitlement.  Circumcision, the first and defining work of the Mosaic Law, functioned as a covenantal identity marker. Many Jewish Christians believed Gentiles must become Jews—enter the covenant through circumcision—to be saved. Paul dismantles this claim. He insists that covenant identity does not justify anyone. Jews and Gentiles stand on equal footing before God, and both are justified by faith in Christ apart from the works that mark Jewish covenant membership. Read in context, “works of law” in Romans 3:28 refers not to good works in general but to the Mosaic Law’s covenant‑defining works. Paul’s point is not “faith alone,” but “no covenant privilege.”  

In the next four chapters, we will examine four arguments showing that “works of law” in Romans 3:28 means **works of the Mosaic Law**.

The first of these is the **Covenant‑Context Argument**. As the name suggests, it is grounded in the immediate context of Romans 3:28—namely, Romans 1–3.

Our method follows the interpretive framework: **RIGHT**—the **R**ules for **I**nterpreting **G**od’s **H**oly **T**ext.

Time to get our Bible on.


## Introduction to the Covenant‑Context Argument

Our focus remains Romans 3:28, where Paul writes:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of law.

As we have already noted, the interpretation of this verse hinges on the meaning of “works of law.” Does Paul mean **good works** (the Protestant reading) or **works of the Mosaic Law** (the Catholic reading)?

Because this is a question of definition, **RIGHT-1** applies:

> **RIGHT‑1**: We must define this expression using its **biblical definition**.

It does not matter how you or I define “works of law.” What matters is how **Paul** uses the expression. And to determine that, we apply **RIGHT-2**:

> **RIGHT‑2**: The interpretation of a verse **must agree with the context**.

Put together, these rules require that we define “works of law” in Romans 3:28 **using the definition supplied by the context**.

Our first step, then, is to examine the *greater context*—Romans 1–3.


## Catholic and Protestant Interpretation of Romans 1–3

Both Protestants and Catholics agree that Paul is asking and answering a crucial question in Romans 1–3. And both agree on the answer:

> All are justified by faith in Christ (Romans 3:28).

Embarrassingly, it is the *question* that we cannot agree on. 

For Protestants, Paul is asking:

> Can a person earn salvation by doing good works?

In this reading, Paul is refuting **moral legalism**—the idea that one can “earn” or “merit” salvation by good works. Protestant theologians often call this error **works‑salvation**, and many accuse Catholics of holding it.

Despite protestations to the contrary, **Catholics firmly reject works‑salvation as heresy**. But Catholics also maintain that this is *not* the issue Paul is addressing in Romans.

For Catholics, Paul’s question is:

> Must a person first become a Jew to be saved?

In this reading, Paul is refuting the **Torah‑based covenantal presumption**—the claim that one must become a Jew and submit to the Mosaic Law to be saved.


## Analysis of the Protestant Reading of Romans 1–3

According to mainline Protestantism, Paul is arguing that no one can “earn” or “merit” salvation.

But how do they arrive at this conclusion? The interpretation is not nearly as straightforward as many assume.

For starters, **there is not a single verse in Romans 1–3 where Paul condemns or even mentions the idea of “earning” salvation**. In fact, Paul seems to lean the other way. He spends a significant portion of Romans 2 *praising* good works:

> #### ✝️ Romans 2:6–7  
> For he [God] will render to every man according to his works. To those who by patience in well‑doing seek for glory and honor and immortality, he will give eternal life.

He adds that there will be “glory and honor and peace for every one who does good, the Jew first and also the Greek,” concluding, “For God shows no partiality” (Romans 2:10–11).

These passages are often dismissed as “hypothetical” by Protestant theologians. But Paul never walks them back.

Yes, Paul later affirms that all are sinners and in need of salvation (Romans 3:9). But that does not nullify his earlier statements. If a person in right standing with God obeys God, is it not God’s sovereign right to reward that obedience?

Protestants often appeal to Romans 2:21–22, where Paul references moral provisions of the Mosaic Law (“Do you steal?” “Do you commit adultery?”). But these verses do not prove Paul is addressing works‑salvation. Paul is simply affirming that one cannot be justified by *evil* works.[^Moral-Provision-1]

No, to make its case, the Protestant position requires Paul to say something like:

> “A person cannot be justified by doing *good works*.”

[^Moral-Provision-1]: The Protestant claim is that **good works do not justify**. “Do you steal?” and “Do you commit adultery?” are examples of breaking the **LAW**, not keeping it. Paul invokes the moral demands of the Mosaic Law to show his Jewish readers that breaking the **LAW** nullifies any covenant advantage they claim. “You who boast in the **LAW**, do you dishonor God by breaking the **LAW**?” (Romans 2:23). If they break the **LAW**, their circumcision becomes uncircumcision (Romans 2:25). In other words, covenant benefits vanish when the covenant is broken.

Does Paul ever do this? Protestants insist he does. 

In Romans 2:25-29 Paul tells his Jewish readers that circumcision—even if it identified them as God’s people and put them in covenant with God—*has no salvific value*. 

Consequently, when Paul later states that man is “justified by faith apart from works of law”, Protests confidently assert this means that man is justified by faith apart from *good works*.

Is this confidence warranted?


## Is Circumcision a “Good” Work?

Circumcision is certainly an odd choice to serve as the poster child for good works in Romans 1-3.

Is it even a “good” work at all? 

Protestant theologians insist that it is *because God ordered it*. But let us at least admit that if it *is* a “good work”, it is still a work that **only Jews do**. Consequently, circumcision lacks the universal scope to allow us to conclude that Paul necessarily had *all good works* in mind when he said “man is justified by faith apart from works of law”.[^Universalize]

[^Universalize]: Many Protestants argue that Paul universalizes the **LAW** in Romans 3:20: “By works of law no flesh will be justified.” They argue: (1) “No flesh” = all humanity. Therefore (2) “works of law” = all works. Premise one is true. “No flesh” does, in fact, mean all humanity. But premise 2 does not follow. “No flesh” universalizes the *subjects*, not the *works*. Paul is saying: “Jews and Gentiles alike are not justified by the Mosaic Law.” In other words, the Mosaic Law is *universally* incapable of justifying *anyone*.

Paul could have said that “loving your neighbor as yourself”—even though commanded by God—is an example of the type of work *that does not justify*. But he never does this. In this context—and in *every* context—the “good work” referenced is circumcision. 

Some Protestants attempt to universalize circumcision by claiming it “implies a desire to live under the demands of the law”[^Life-Application]. Circumcision is likewise heralded as a placeholder *for any and all good works*. 

[^Life-Application]: Bruce B. Barton et al., eds., *Life Application New Testament Commentary* (Wheaton, IL: Tyndale House Publishers, 2001), p. 595.
The authors posit a position held by many Protestants: Obedience is obedience; if circumcision doesn’t justify, no obedience does. But circumcision is not a *moral* act; it is a *covenantal sign*. Paul’s argument is about covenant membership, not moral merit.

But it would seem a poor fit. The Mosaic Law commanded that male Jewish children be circumcised on the eighth day after birth. That’s not a *moral* decision the child makes. And there is certainly no conscious “desire to live under the demands of the law”[^Life-Application].

And yet, Paul tells us that the Jews *boasted* about being circumcised. Why? Because they were taking credit for some quasi-moral work that they did not consent to or remember receiving? Surely not. 

Circumcision simply does not work as a representative moral act. Paul’s point was something else entirely.

What was Paul’s point? 

**That Jewish circumcision was not a *moral* act; it was, instead, an act of *covenant identity*.**


## The Covenant Context

Because of God’s covenant with their forefathers, Jews viewed themselves as God’s people. The Old Testament repeatedly reinforces this identity.

And how did one enter the covenant? *Through circumcision*.

A covenant is a sacred contract involving the exchange of persons: God gives Himself to a people; the people give themselves to God.

**Question**: When does a contract go into force? 

**Answer**: When you *sign* it. 

And that’s what the Jews did. Only they didn’t put their mark on a piece of paper; **they marked their flesh**.  

Circumcision was the first and preeminent work of the Mosaic Law. It functioned as a **covenant identity marker** defining the Jews’ unique place as God’s own people. 


## A House Divided

This Jew–Gentile tension is not unique to Romans 1-3. It appears throughout the New Testament—in Galatians 2–3, Ephesians 2, Acts 10–11, Acts 15, Philippians 3, and elsewhere—showing that the early Church struggled with the question of Gentile inclusion.

This divide was clearly manifested in the fact that many first‑century Jewish Christians did not accept the Gentiles as brothers and sisters in Christ. This tension forms the backdrop of Paul’s argument in Romans 1-3.

Over a dozen times Paul contrasts “Jews/Gentiles”, “Jews/Greeks”, “circumcised/ uncircumcised”,  those “under the **LAW**/without the **LAW**”. Paul even names his interlocutor: “You call yourself a **Jew** and rely upon the **LAW** and boast of your relation to God...” (Romans 2:17).

Paul aims to strip his Jewish readers of any pretense of covenant privilege writing:

- “**God shows no partiality**” (Romans 2:11)
- “**all men, both Jews and Greeks, are under the power of sin**” (Romans 3:9)
- “**there is no distinction; since all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God**” (Romans 3:22-23)

Plainly stated: The problem wasn’t **merit**; it was **entitlement**.


## Conclusion

While the Protestant reading struggles to find a sure footing in Romans 1-3, the Catholic covenantal interpretation is difficult to miss. Throw a dart at Romans 2 and it would be hard to hit a verse that did *not* deal with Jewish covenant inclusion and identity. 

This is the context in which we must read Romans 3:28. Everything leading up to this verse—and the verse itself—is about **covenant identity**:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of **LAW**.

That is not Paul teaching justification by faith alone. It is Paul teaching that Jews have **no privilege based on the Mosaic Law**.

The Old Covenant has been set aside. Under the New Covenant, Jews and Gentiles have *equal* access to God—**by faith**.

That is how Romans 3:28 must be read—firmly anchored to its context.

What happens if we fail do this (as many have)? 

Simply put: The moment you divorce a verse like Romans 3:28 from its *context*, you divorce it from its *meaning* (**RIGHT‑2**).

And this, we must not do.

Now, I recognize that the **Covenant‑Context Argument**, while strong, is also a context‑dependent argument. There is a degree of subjectivity to it. So if you’re not fully persuaded yet, that’s understandable.

Rest assured, though: much better and far stronger arguments await.
