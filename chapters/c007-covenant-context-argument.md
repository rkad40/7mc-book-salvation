# The Covenant‑Context Argument (Romans 1:1–3:27)

> #### 📄 Abstract
> Paul’s argument in Romans 1–3 is not about people trying to earn salvation by good works. It is about Jews claiming covenant privilege through the Mosaic Law—especially circumcision. Throughout these chapters, Paul contrasts Jew/Gentile, circumcised/uncircumcised, and those under the **LAW**/without the **LAW**. His target is not moral legalism but covenantal entitlement. Circumcision, the first and defining work of the Mosaic Law, functioned as a covenant identity marker. Many Jewish Christians believed Gentiles must become Jews—enter the covenant through circumcision—to be saved. Paul dismantles this claim. He insists that covenant identity does not justify anyone. Jews and Gentiles stand on equal footing before God, and both are justified by faith in Christ apart from the works that mark Jewish covenant membership. Read in context, “works of law” in Romans 3:28 refers not to good works in general but to the Mosaic Law’s covenant‑defining works. Paul’s point is not “faith alone,” but “no covenant privilege.”

The first of the four arguments we will consider is the **Covenant‑Context Argument**. As the name suggests, it is grounded in the immediate context of Romans 3:28—namely, Romans 1–3.

Our method follows the interpretive framework **RIGHT**—the **R**ules for **I**nterpreting **G**od’s **H**oly **T**ext.

Time to get our Bible on.


## Introduction to the Covenant‑Context Argument

Our focus remains Romans 3:28:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of law.

As we have already noted, the interpretation of this verse hinges on the meaning of “works of law.” There are two possibilities:

- “works of law” = **good works** (the Protestant reading)
- “works of law” = **works of the Mosaic Law** (the Catholic reading)

Once again, it does not matter how you or I define “works of law.” **What matters is how Paul defines this expression.**

So how do we determine what Paul had in mind?

The **RIGHT** rules I championed in the last chapter tell us that we don’t need to guess or flip a coin. **We simply have to appeal to the context.**


## Catholic and Protestant Interpretation of Romans 1–3

Both Protestants and Catholics agree that Paul is asking and answering a crucial question in Romans 1–3. And both agree the answer is found in Romans 3:28:

**Justification comes through faith in Christ.**

Embarrassingly, it is the **question** that we cannot agree on. 

For Protestants, Paul is asking:

**“Can a person earn salvation by doing good works?”**

In this reading, Paul is refuting **moral legalism**—the idea that one can “earn” or “merit” salvation by good works. This is the classic **works‑salvation heresy**, which many Protestants accuse Catholics of holding.

Despite protestations to the contrary, **Catholics firmly reject works‑salvation as heresy**.[^Pelagian] But Catholics also maintain that this is **not** the issue Paul is addressing in Romans.

[^Pelagian]: Pelagianism is the 5th‑century teaching of Pelagius that denied original sin and claimed that human beings, by their natural powers, can choose the good, fulfill God’s commandments, and attain salvation through their own merit and without the necessity of interior divine grace. It was condemned because it made grace optional rather than essential, reducing Christ’s work to moral example rather than transformative aid.

For Catholics, the question Paul is answering is:

**“Must a person first become a Jew to be saved?”**

In this reading, Paul is refuting **Torah‑based covenantal exclusivism**—sometimes called the **Judaizer heresy**. This is the biblically attested claim that some believed one must become a Jew to be saved (Acts 15).


## Analysis of the Protestant Reading of Romans 1–3

According to mainline Protestantism, Paul is arguing that no one can “earn” or “merit” salvation.

But Paul says nothing of the kind.

In fact, **there is not a single verse in Romans 1–3 where Paul condemns—or even mentions—the idea of earning salvation**.

If anything, Paul leans the other way. He spends a significant portion of Romans 2 **praising** good works:

> #### ✝️ Romans 2:6–7  
> For he [God] will render to every man according to his works. To those who by patience in well‑doing seek for glory and honor and immortality, he will give eternal life.

Paul adds that there will be “glory and honor and peace **for everyone who does good**, the Jew first and also the Greek,” concluding, “For God shows no partiality” (Romans 2:10–11).

Protestants often dismiss these verses as **“hypothetical.”** But Paul never signals that he is speaking hypothetically, nor does he ever walk the statements back.

Yes, Paul later affirms that all are sinners (Romans 3:9). **But this does not nullify what he has already said.** If a person in right standing with God obeys God, is it not God’s sovereign right to reward that obedience—even with “eternal life”?

Protestants sometimes  appeal to Romans 2:21–22, where Paul references moral provisions of the Mosaic Law (“Do you steal?” “Do you commit adultery?”). But these verses say nothing about earning salvation through human effort. **At most, they refute justification by *evil* works, not justification by *good* ones**.[^Moral-Provision-1]

[^Moral-Provision-1]: The Protestant claim is that **good works do not justify**. “Do you steal?” and “Do you commit adultery?” are examples of breaking the **LAW**, not keeping it. Paul invokes the moral demands of the Mosaic Law to show his Jewish readers that breaking the **LAW** nullifies any covenant advantage they claim. “You who boast in the **LAW**, do you dishonor God by breaking the **LAW**?” (Romans 2:23). If they break the **LAW**, their circumcision becomes uncircumcision (Romans 2:25). In other words, covenant benefits vanish when the covenant is broken.

No, for the Protestant reading to work, Paul would need to say something like:

> “A person cannot be justified by doing **good works**.”

But Paul never says this—or anything remotely similar.

Instead, Protestants point to Romans 2:25–29, where Paul tells his Jewish readers that circumcision—even though it identified them as God’s people and placed them in covenant with God—**has no salvific value**.

But is **that** the smoking gun Protestants think it is?


## Is Circumcision a “Good” Work?

Circumcision is certainly an odd choice to serve as the poster child for good works in Romans 1–3.

One could rightly ask: **Is circumcision even a *good* work at all?** 

Protestant theologians insist that it is—**because God commanded it**. But let us at least admit that if it *is* a “good work”, it is still a work that **only Jews do**. Consequently, circumcision lacks the universal scope to allow us to conclude that Paul necessarily had **all good works** in mind when he said “man is justified by faith apart from works of law”.[^Universalize]

[^Universalize]: Many Protestants argue that Paul universalizes the **LAW** in Romans 3:20: “By works of law no flesh will be justified.” They reason: (1) “No flesh” = all humanity. Therefore (2) “works of law” = all works. Premise one is true. “No flesh” does indeed mean all humanity. But premise two does not follow. “No flesh” universalizes the *subjects*, not the *works*. Paul is saying: “Jews and Gentiles alike are not justified by the Mosaic Law.” In other words, the Mosaic Law is *universally* incapable of justifying *anyone*.

If Paul wanted to make that point, he could have chosen a universal moral act—**“love your neighbor,”** **“feed the poor,”** **“forgive your enemy.”** But he never does. The only “work” in view is **circumcision**.

That, by itself, should be telling.

Some Protestants attempt to universalize circumcision by claiming it “implies a desire to live under the demands of the law.”[^Life-Application] In this view, circumcision becomes a placeholder **for any and all good works**.

[^Life-Application]: Bruce B. Barton et al., eds., *Life Application New Testament Commentary* (Wheaton, IL: Tyndale House Publishers, 2001), p. 595. The authors posit a position held by many Protestants: obedience is obedience; if circumcision doesn’t justify, no obedience does. But circumcision is not a *moral* act; it is a *covenantal sign*. Paul’s argument is about covenant membership, not moral merit.

But this seems a poor fit. The Mosaic Law commanded that male infants be circumcised on the eighth day. That is not a **moral** decision the child makes. And there is certainly no conscious “desire to live under the demands of the law.”[^Life-Application]

And yet Paul tells us that the Jews **boasted** in circumcision. Why? Because they were taking credit for some quasi‑moral work they neither consented to nor remembered receiving?

Surely not. 

Circumcision simply does not function as a representative moral act. Paul’s point was something else entirely.

What, then, was Paul’s point? 

**Jewish circumcision did not signal *moral* merit; it signaled *covenant identity*.**


## The Covenant Context

Because of God’s covenant with their forefathers, **Jews viewed themselves as God’s people**. The Old Testament reinforces this identity again and again.[^God’s-People]

[^God’s-People]: See Exodus 6:7; 19:5–6; Leviticus 26:12; Genesis 17:7–8; Deuteronomy 7:6–8; 14:2; 26:17–19; 2 Samuel 7:23–24; 1 Kings 8:51–53; 2 Chronicles 7:14; Isaiah 43:1; 43:20–21; Jeremiah 7:23; 30:22; 31:33; Ezekiel 36:28; 37:27; Hosea 2:23; Joel 2:26–27; Micah 6:3–5.

And how did one enter the covenant? 

**Through *circumcision*.**

The Bible defines a covenant as a sacred contract involving the exchange of persons. In the Old Covenant formulation: God gives Himself to a people; the people give themselves to God. 

If you are reading this book, I’m guessing you understand the concept of a contract. You may even have dealt with your fair share of them. A question, then:

**When does a contract go into effect?** 

Answer: 

**When you *sign* it.**

And for the record, that is exactly what the Jews did. Only they didn’t put their mark on a piece of paper; **they marked their flesh**. That is was circumcision was—the sacred signing of the covenant contract. 

Circumcision was the first and foundational work of the Mosaic Law. It functioned as a **covenant identity marker**, defining the Jews’ unique place as God’s own people. 


## A House Divided

The Jew–Gentile tension described in Romans 1–3 is not unique to Romans. It appears throughout the New Testament. Here is a small sample of prominent instances:

- **Galatians 2–3**
- **Ephesians 2**
- **Acts 10–11**
- **Acts 15**
- **Philippians 3** 

It is no exaggeration to say that the early Church was a house divided over the question of **Gentile inclusion**. This divide was clearly manifested in the fact that **many first‑century Jewish Christians did not accept Gentiles as brothers and sisters in Christ**—a tension that forms the backdrop of Paul’s argument in Romans 1–3.

Over a dozen times Paul contrasts “Jews” / “Gentiles,” “Jews” / “Greeks,” “circumcised” / “uncircumcised,” and those “under the **LAW**” / “without the **LAW**.” Paul even names his interlocutor: “You call yourself a **Jew** and rely upon the **LAW** and boast of your relation to God...” (Romans 2:17).

Pointedly, Paul aims to strip his Jewish readers of any pretense of covenant privilege. He writes:

- “**God shows no partiality**” (Romans 2:11)
- “**all men, both Jews and Greeks, are under the power of sin**” (Romans 3:9)
- “**there is no distinction; since all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God**” (Romans 3:22–23)

Plainly stated: 

**The problem of Romans 1–3 isn’t *merit*; it is *entitlement*.**

In the final analysis, Romans 1–3 does not support the idea that Paul was arguing against the **works‑salvation heresy**. He was, rather, taking square aim at the **Judaizer heresy**—the idea that Gentile Christians were not already saved because they had not submitted to the Law of Moses.


## Conclusion

While the Protestant reading struggles to find a sure footing in Romans 1–3, the Catholic covenantal interpretation is difficult to miss. Throw a dart at Romans 2 and it would be hard to hit a verse that does *not* deal with Jewish covenant inclusion and identity.

This is the context in which we must read Romans 3:28. Everything leading up to this verse—and the verse itself—is about **covenant identity**:

> #### ✝️ Romans 3:28 (RSV‑2CE)
> For we hold that a man is justified by faith apart from works of **LAW**.

That is not Paul teaching justification by faith alone. It is Paul teaching that Jews have **no covenant privilege based on the Mosaic Law**.

The Old Covenant has been set aside. Under the New Covenant, Jews and Gentiles stand on **equal** footing before God—**justified by faith**.

Romans 3:28 must be read in that light—firmly anchored to its covenant context.

Now, I recognize that the **Covenant‑Context Argument**, while strong, is also a context‑dependent argument. There is a degree of subjectivity to it. So if you’re not fully persuaded yet, that’s understandable.

Rest assured, though: much better and far stronger arguments await.
