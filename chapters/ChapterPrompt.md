# Book Context and Reusable Chapter-Review Prompt

## Book Goal

This book examines the Catholic doctrine of salvation and justification in conversation with **classical confessional Reformed Protestantism**. Its central organizing question is the Philippian jailer’s question in Acts 16:30:

> “What must I do to be saved?”

The book is written from a Catholic perspective, but its purpose is not merely to defend a predetermined conclusion. It seeks to:

- articulate Catholic and Reformed positions accurately and charitably;
- identify their genuine agreements and disagreements;
- examine the biblical and logical premises underlying both positions;
- answer common Reformed objections to Catholic teaching;
- correct caricatures, especially the formula that Catholicism teaches salvation by unaided “faith and works”;
- invite readers to follow Christ and the truth with humility, prayer, and a well-formed conscience.

The desired result is an accessible Catholic apologetic that takes Reformed arguments seriously, acknowledges valid Protestant insights, and does not claim more than the evidence supports.

## Scope

The Protestant Reformation provides the historical setting, and Martin Luther may be discussed as its principal catalyst. Luther should not, however, be called “proto-Reformed.” Lutheranism and Reformed Protestantism became distinct theological traditions.

The book’s principal Protestant conversation partner is:

> **Classical confessional Reformed Protestantism, represented especially by the Westminster Confession and Catechisms.**

Unless context indicates otherwise, shorthand references to “the Protestant position” generally mean this Reformed position. When another tradition differs materially—Lutheran, Anglican, Anabaptist, Wesleyan/Arminian, or otherwise—the distinction should be noted briefly where relevant.

The Reformed position should be defined primarily through its confessional sources. Individual theologians such as Calvin, R. C. Sproul, Michael Horton, John Piper, or others may illustrate the position, but personalities should not replace authoritative confessional definitions.

## Intended Audience

The intended audience includes:

- Catholics who want to understand and explain their faith more accurately;
- Protestants, especially Reformed Protestants, who want a serious account of Catholic teaching;
- Christians unfamiliar with the technical debates over justification;
- interested readers who value short, approachable chapters rather than academic monographs.

Do not assume that readers already understand specialized terms. Explain terms such as *justification*, *sanctifying grace*, *imputation*, *infusion*, *forensic*, *mortal sin*, and *sola fide* when they first become important.

## Format and Reading Length

Each chapter promises a read of no more than approximately seven minutes. The abstract, footnotes, Scripture quotations, and “Ask an AI” section are excluded from that calculation.

The seven-minute label is a limiting editorial standard, not a claim that every reader will finish at exactly the same speed. Preserve concision and avoid making each chapter answer every possible objection. Earlier arguments may be summarized and linked rather than re-proved.

The book will be published online and will be interactive. The abstract is initially hidden so that readers may open it for a synopsis and decide whether to read or skip the chapter.

## Voice and Tone

Preserve the author’s voice. It should be:

- informative;
- pastoral;
- charitable;
- confident without being triumphalistic;
- slightly informal;
- occasionally witty or playful;
- spiritually serious without becoming ponderous.

Well-placed levity is welcome when it clarifies an idea, relieves density, or makes the chapter memorable. Humor should not make the opposing side appear foolish, ignorant, dishonest, or unwelcome.

Visceral language may be retained when it serves a deliberate purpose. For example, “Embarrassingly” is used in Chapter 1 to convey that Christian division over salvation should trouble Christians and motivate honest engagement. Do not automatically flatten purposeful rhetoric into bland language.

## Charity Standards

Apply the principle of charitable representation:

1. State an opponent’s position in terms its informed adherents could recognize.
2. Distinguish official doctrine from popular expressions.
3. Distinguish a theological system from the spiritual state of individual adherents.
4. Acknowledge genuine common ground before identifying disagreement.
5. Avoid universal claims such as “all Protestants” unless the evidence truly warrants them.
6. Avoid attributing dishonest motives when an exegetical or logical mistake is sufficient to explain the disagreement.
7. Permit firm conclusions when the chapter has established them, but qualify contextual inference, scholarly dispute, and argument from silence appropriately.

Charity does not require suppressing real disagreement. The book may plainly identify contradictions, weak premises, circular arguments, historical inaccuracies, or consequences of a position. It should criticize ideas rather than belittle people.

## Accuracy Standards

Check factual claims involving:

- Scripture and its immediate literary context;
- Greek or Hebrew vocabulary;
- Catholic doctrine and the *Catechism of the Catholic Church*;
- Reformed doctrine and the Westminster Standards;
- Lutheran doctrine and the Book of Concord when Luther or Lutheranism is discussed;
- Church history and the development of doctrine;
- named theologians and quotations;
- ecumenical documents such as the *Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification* and *Evangelicals and Catholics Together*.

Prefer primary and authoritative sources. Do not silently treat a disputed interpretation as an uncontested lexical or historical fact.

Important distinctions already established include:

- Ancient apostolic churches shared a broadly sacramental and transformative understanding of salvation, but they did not agree on every detail.
- Early Christological schisms had profound implications for salvation, though they did not center on the later *sola fide* controversy.
- Catholic justification includes forgiveness, sanctification, and interior renewal; initial justification is ordinarily conferred in Baptism.
- In an adult convert, grace precedes faith and Baptism. Infant Baptism requires a separate explanation because personal profession follows rather than precedes the sacrament.
- Classical Reformed justification is forensic; Christ’s righteousness is imputed and received through faith alone.
- In Reformed theology, works are fruits and evidence of saving faith, not the ground or instrument of justification.
- Perseverance of the saints is characteristically Reformed. Confessional Lutherans allow that a believer can fall away from saving faith.
- Luther was a catalyst of the Reformation, not a “proto-Reformed” theologian.

## Editorial Workflow

Unless the user explicitly authorizes changes, begin with a **read-only planning review**. Do not edit the chapter during that stage.

For each chapter:

1. Read the entire chapter and the immediately preceding chapter when continuity matters.
2. Identify the chapter’s central claim and argumentative structure.
3. List grammar, spelling, punctuation, and Markdown problems.
4. Evaluate clarity, transitions, and logical flow.
5. Fact-check historical, biblical, lexical, doctrinal, and attributed claims.
6. Test premises against the strongest likely Reformed objection.
7. Explain whether an objection merely weakens one supporting reason or invalidates the central conclusion.
8. Evaluate tone and identify any uncharitable or overbroad wording.
9. Identify any overstatement and propose a narrower, more defensible formulation.
10. Suggest opportunities for concise levity when appropriate.
11. Grade the chapter and explain both its strengths and weaknesses.
12. Wait for discussion and explicit authorization before editing.
13. After authorization, apply only the agreed changes, preserve the author’s voice, and verify the finished Markdown.

Do not force each chapter to restate premises that earlier chapters have already established. If a conclusion depends on an earlier argument, determine whether a short reminder or link is sufficient.

## Reusable Prompt

Copy and paste the following prompt when beginning or resuming a chapter review:

---

I am writing an online Catholic book about salvation and justification, organized around the question, “What must I do to be saved?” Its principal theological comparison is between Catholicism and **classical confessional Reformed Protestantism**, especially the Westminster tradition. Martin Luther and other Protestant traditions may appear for historical context, but do not treat Luther as Reformed or assume that Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Anabaptist, and Wesleyan positions are interchangeable.

The book seeks to articulate both Catholic and Reformed positions accurately, test their biblical and logical premises, answer Reformed objections to Catholic teaching, and invite readers to pursue truth with humility and prayer. It is written from a Catholic perspective but should acknowledge common ground and valid Protestant insights.

Please review the active chapter as follows:

1. Correct grammar, spelling, punctuation, and Markdown problems.
2. Check clarity and flow. Identify the central point and whether it is communicated clearly.
3. Check biblical, historical, lexical, and doctrinal accuracy using authoritative primary sources where appropriate.
4. Check logical consistency. Identify assumptions, non sequiturs, circular arguments, false dilemmas, or conclusions stronger than their premises.
5. Test the argument against the strongest likely Reformed response. Explain whether each objection weakens a supporting reason or invalidates the main conclusion.
6. Check the tone for charity. Preserve firm disagreement, but avoid caricature, unnecessary contempt, and claims about motives.
7. Identify places where I have overstepped and suggest narrower, defensible language.
8. Preserve my voice: informative, pastoral, charitable, slightly informal, and occasionally witty.
9. Suggest well-placed levity where it would improve readability without making the opposing side the butt of the joke.
10. Grade the chapter and explain where it succeeds and where it remains weak.

Each chapter should remain roughly within a seven-minute read, excluding the abstract, footnotes, Scripture quotations, and “Ask an AI” section. Do not make the chapter answer every possible objection if earlier or later chapters can carry that work.

This is initially a **read-only planning review**. Do not modify the file until I explicitly approve the proposed changes. Once I authorize editing, apply the agreed changes directly, preserve my voice, and perform a final Markdown and consistency check.
