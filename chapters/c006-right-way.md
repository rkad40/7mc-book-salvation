Before we jump into the formal arguments that I teased in the last chapter, there is a bit of housekeeping that we must deal with first.

One of the sad truths of Christianity is that, although Christians around the globe draw their inspiration from the same inspired Text we call the Bible, the different interpretations we derive from it are seemingly without limit. Worse yet, no matter how many interpretation there are, you are going to have proponents of each that will tell you their interpretation is the “clear” meaning of Scripture. 

How did we arrive at this place in Christianity? Why are there so many interpretations of Scripture out there?

The answers are varied and beyond the scope of a 7-minute chapter. But I think one of the biggest problems comes down to two simple questions:

1. Do you have a sound set of exegetical rules?
2. Do you follow them?

Having a good set of rules for interpreting the Bible and sticking by them—even if you may not like the direction they are pointing—is the critical.  

In this chapter, I am going to share mine.  


## RIGHT: Rules for Interpreting God’s Holy Text

One of the best defenses we have against drifting into heretical error is to employ sound exegetical rules. Applied correctly, rules become tools that keep us from importing our own assumptions, preferences, and modern meanings into the sacred Text.

While there are many rules one might reasonably apply, there are two that are absolutely critical. I collectively call this two‑rule system **RIGHT**—short for **R**ules for **I**nterpreting **G**od’s **H**oly **T**ext. 

### RIGHT‑1: You must define biblical words and expressions using their *biblical* definition.  

This first rule seems obvious, but it is astonishing how often it is ignored. **It is interpretively illicit to import our own definition of a biblical word or expression into the sacred Text**. If we want to know what Saint Paul means by a verse like Romans 3:28, we had better define words like “justified,” “faith,” and “works of law” in the way *Paul* defines them.

Often you will encounter a passage that various Christian traditions interpret differently. One interpretive method I frequently employ to evaluate which interpretation is correct is what I call **BYOD Test**—**B**ring **Y**our **O**wn **D**efinition.

The **BYOD Test** is an extension of **RIGHT-1**—making sure we define biblical words and expressions using their biblical meaning. To apply the **BYOD Test**, simply replace the key words in the verse with the definitions used by different traditions. Very often, you will see that the correct interpretation becomes obvious. One reading will fit naturally; the other will look like someone trying to shove the square pegs of their theology through the round holes of Scripture.


### RIGHT‑2: Any interpretation of a verse that does not agree with the *context* of the verse is wrong.

Again, this might seem otherwise obvious. But Christian apologists break this rule with alarming frequency. 

At issue is the fact that **the Bible is not a collection of dogmatic one‑liners**. It is, rather, the living, breathing Word of God. The popular tactic of “verse slinging” may *seem* authoritative because it involves quoting the Word of God. But the moment you divorce a verse from its **context**, you invariably divorce it from its **meaning**.

It is a little problem I have coined **soundbite theology**. 

You will invariably encounter people that try to build theology on a single verse, or worse yet on select group of words in a verse. One cannot expect to arrive at the correct understanding of the Scripture by doing this. 

When defining a biblical word or expression using context, there is a clear interpretive hierarchy:

> **Immediate > Chapter > Book > Bible**

This simply means that if the immediate context defines a word, we cannot override that definition by importing a different meaning from 185 chapters away in another book of the Bible. (If that number seems oddly specific, you’ll soon see why.)


### RIGHT‑0: The Rule to Rule All Rules

Often these two rules dovetail into a single *super‑rule*. That happens when the definition of a key word or expression is actually included in the immediate context. When this occurs, I refer to it as **RIGHT‑0**.
