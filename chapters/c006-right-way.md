## RIGHT: Rules for Interpreting God’s Holy Text

One of the best defenses we have against drifting into heretical error is to employ sound exegetical rules. Applied correctly, rules become tools that keep us from importing our own assumptions, preferences, and modern meanings into the sacred Text.

While there are many rules one might reasonably apply, there are two that are absolutely critical. I collectively call this two‑rule system **RIGHT**—short for **R**ules for **I**nterpreting **G**od’s **H**oly **T**ext. 


### RIGHT‑1: You must define biblical words and expressions using their *biblical* definition.  

This first rule seems obvious, but it is astonishing how often it is ignored. **It is interpretively illicit to import our own definition of a biblical word or expression into the sacred Text**. If we want to know what Saint Paul means by a verse like Romans 3:28, we had better define words like “justified,” “faith,” and “works of law” in the way *Paul* defines them.

Often you will encounter a passage that various Christian traditions interpret differently. One interpretive method I frequently employ to evaluate which interpretation is correct is what I call **BYOD Test**—**B**ring **Y**our **O**wn **D**efinition.

The **BYOD Test** is an extension of **RIGHT-1**—making sure we define biblical words and expressions using their biblical meaning. To apply the **BYOD Test**, simply replace the key words in the verse with the definitions used by different traditions. Very often, you will see that the correct interpretation becomes obvious. One reading will fit naturally; the other will look like someone trying to shove the square pegs of their theology through the round holes of Scripture.


### RIGHT‑2: Any interpretation of a verse that does not agree with the *context* of the verse is wrong.

Context is key when attempting to interpret any verse. If your interpretation of the verse does not sit well in the context, it is a sure sign that it is time to get a new interpretation. 

Taking verses out of context, you an literally make the Bible say anything you want it to:

- Angry at your parents and want some biblical validation? -- *There is a verse for that. (Luke 14:26)*
- Think we should bring back corporeal punishment of children? -- *There is a verse for that. (Proverbs 13:24)*
- Need a biblical precedent for slurring someone you don't like? -- *There is a verse for that. (Matthew 7:6)*
- Want to engage in self mutilation? -- *There is a verse for that. (Matthew 5:29)*
- A fan of infanticide? -- *There is a verse for that. (Psalm 137:9)*
- Cannibalism something you can get onboard with? -- *There is a verse for that. (John 6:53)*

And it's not these over-the-top examples that are the true problem. The true problem is the subtle misinterpretations that cumulatively lead to serious error.  

It is not uncommon these days to hear Christian apologists simply shotgun verses against an opponent without commentary--as if the sheer number of biblical citations create an impenetrable wall against which the adversary's logical attack with be blunted.   

Some call this tactic “verse slinging”. My own pet name for it is far moore appropriate. I call it **soundbite theology**. 

Brandishing verses in this manner certainly **seems** authoritative because it involves quoting the Word of God which **is** authoritative. The problem is, the moment you divorce a verse from its **context**, you invariably divorce it from its **meaning**.

Reason being: **The Bible is not a collection of dogmatic one‑liners**; it is, rather, the **living, breathing Word of God**. 

The way that we avoid engaging in soundbite theology is to make sure that when we do cite a verse and appeal to it **clear** meaning, that its assessed meaning is **clearly** taught by the context.  

Now, context has a pecking order that goes like this:

> **Immediate > Chapter > Book > Bible**

This says that immediate context carries the most weight, followed by the the parent chapter, then book, and finally the whole of Scripture itself.

For example, if we are trying to arrive at the correct definition of a biblical word on which the meaning a particular verse hangs, and the word is formally defined in the previous verse, we cannot appeal to a different definition of the same word one hundred and eighty-five chapters away.
