# A Question of Salvation

> #### 📄 Abstract
> The book opens with the Philippian jailer’s urgent question to Paul and Silas: “What must I do to be saved?” From there, it argues that no question carries greater consequence than salvation. Because Catholics and Protestants answer this question differently, the chapter invites readers into a careful, prayerful examination of the dispute from a Catholic perspective, while asking all Christians to follow Christ wherever the truth leads.

Our story begins in Acts 16:16. Let us set the stage...

Paul—the self-described “apostle to the Gentiles”—and his companions have traveled to Philippi to preach the gospel. While there, Paul frees an enslaved girl from a spirit of divination. When her owners realize that their source of profit is gone, they seize Paul and his missionary companion, Silas, and drag them before the magistrates. The two are accused of “disturbing our city,” beaten without a trial, thrown into the inner prison, and fastened in stocks.

Paul and Silas are undeterred. The other prisoners can hear them praying and singing.

Around midnight, a violent earthquake shakes the prison to its foundations. The doors fly open, and every prisoner’s chains come loose.

The jailer, woken by the earthquake, rushes to the scene. When he sees the prison doors open, he draws his sword to kill himself. Such an act today might seem extreme. But Philippi was a Roman colony, and the rule of Rome was absolute. If a prisoner escaped a guard’s custody, the guard received the prisoner’s punishment.

Before the man can fall upon his own blade, Paul intervenes. “Do not harm yourself; we are all here,” he says. When the jailer enters the prison and finds all its occupants unchained, he falls at the feet of Paul and Silas. In great fear, he asks:

> #### ✝️ Acts 16:30
> “What must I do to be saved?”

Credit the jailer for asking the right question at the right time.

He does not ask:

“How did you unchain yourselves?” 

or: 

“What does this great sign mean?” 

No, his question cuts to the core of it: 

**“What must I do to be saved?”** 


## What is More Important Than Our Salvation?

The question is a timeless one. It has been asked countless times over the last two thousand years.

Giving proper gravity to this question, a second usually comes to mind:

**What is more important than our salvation?**

Is health and well-being more important? Fortune or fame? What about happiness? Or fulfillment?

No—all of these things are insignificant when measured against the importance of salvation. For in salvation, we find **infinite consequence**.

Jesus Christ, the God‑man and great Architect of salvation, taught:

> #### ✝️ Mark 8:36
> “For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his life?”

You can be the most popular person to have ever lived, the richest, the most beloved. They can build statues of you, name cities after you, and speak your name to the end of time with the utmost reverence. But if all of this comes at the expense of your salvation, then it was all a pointless waste.


## The Salvation Controversy

Hopefully you agree with all of this. I assume that if you are reading this book, you do. There is no greater question any of us can ask than, **“What must I do to be saved?”** It is such an important question that we must leave no stone unturned.

Unfortunately, it is also a question that divides Christians today. 

Embarrassingly, although Catholics and Protestants share foundational beliefs about Christ and grace, they remain divided over important aspects of how salvation is received, understood, and lived. The dispute has divided churches, families, and nations, sometimes with tragic consequences. We cannot hope to resolve that division until we understand it. The first step is to articulate clearly—and charitably—what each side actually believes.

In this book, we are going to take a fresh look at this question. Where do we see harmony? Where is there division? Is there any way to arrive at the correct answer?

Spoiler alert: I am a Catholic, and I will be approaching this question from a Catholic perspective. 

Even if you are not Catholic, and even if you do not agree with the Church’s teachings on this matter, I hope you will agree that this is an important discussion to have. Perhaps some insight you glean from this text will help you better understand—and, where necessary, challenge—Catholics like myself. Or perhaps this text will reveal to you more clearly the mystery of God’s plan of salvation.

Like you, I am a human being, capable of persisting in error. If my arguments are invalid, I *want* to know. 

If I arrived at my current position thinking Christ to be *here*, only later to discover that he is not here but *there*, then my path is clear. If I fail to go where I now know he is—if I fail on account of pride, social moorings, or any other entanglements that hinder my desire—then can I call myself a follower of Christ at all?

To be a follower of Christ means *to go where he is*. To do less is to say, by action, that I do not regard the question **“What must I do to be saved?”** with the utmost importance.

This duty belongs to all of us. It does not mean that every sincere reader will reach the same conclusion at the same time. It means that each of us must be willing to follow Christ wherever truth and a well-formed conscience lead. As we move forward in this book, I ask you to keep this prayerfully in mind.

We ask this in your holy name.**
